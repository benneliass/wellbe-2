# Key findings

1. Missed or delayed identification commonly occurs at handoff points: referrals, results, follow-up, ED discharge, public/private transitions, and repeated visits.
2. High-resource systems still experience diagnostic harm because continuity, workload, IT surfacing, and follow-up ownership fail.
3. Low-resource systems face additional constraints: diagnostic capacity, travel, cost, pathology/imaging access, and referral completion.
4. Cultural safety, racism, language access, and trust directly affect whether symptoms are heard, believed, escalated, and followed up.
5. Community health workers and informal providers can be either blind spots or valuable early-identification nodes, depending on whether they are connected to formal referral/testing systems.
6. Product design must adapt to local system type: GP-gatekeeper, open-access specialist, insurance-network, integrated health plan, CHW-based, or fragmented public/private/informal systems.
