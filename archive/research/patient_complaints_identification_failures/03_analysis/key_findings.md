# Key findings

1. Patient complaints often reveal diagnostic workflow failures that are not visible in ordinary clinical records.
2. The most common black zones in this research were: interrupted patient history, normal-test false reassurance, abnormal results not followed up, referral delays or wrong-specialty routing, and repeated visits not connected.
3. Patient language such as “dismissed,” “not listened to,” “too young,” “normal,” and “still no idea” often maps to concrete process failures.
4. Official cases show that result follow-up requires named ownership; simply receiving, viewing, or filing a result is not enough.
5. Positive examples repeatedly include time, listening, explanation, whole-person context, and continuity.
6. Low-resource settings add additional layers: test reliability, equipment validity, affordability, and availability of specialist referral.
