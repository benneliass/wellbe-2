# Research E main report

## Summary

This research examined how health issue identification differs across health systems, cultures, and resource levels. The central finding is that missed or delayed identification usually happens at handoff points: repeat visits, after-hours care, abnormal-result follow-up, public/private transitions, specialist referral queues, language/cultural interpretation, and rural/low-resource escalation.

## Scope

The work compared the US, UK, Israel, Europe, India, East Asia, Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa, rural high-income settings, rural low-income settings, Indigenous/underserved communities, migrant/refugee health settings, public vs private systems, insurance-based vs universal systems, hospital-first vs GP-gatekeeper systems, and community-health-worker systems.

## Method

The completed research synthesized public health-system profiles, patient-safety investigations, ombudsman reports, peer-reviewed studies, government reports, WHO/OECD/AHRQ material, and implementation examples. This packaging step did not add new research.

## Source overview

The source base includes 30 sources. Most are high-quality institutional or peer-reviewed sources. Medium-quality sources were retained where they provide important cultural, local, or implementation detail.

## Key findings

- Diagnosis is a process across people, settings, records, tests, and time.
- High-resource systems still miss diagnoses through continuity and follow-up failures.
- Low-resource systems add barriers of cost, distance, diagnostic capacity, and referral completion.
- Cultural safety, language access, racism, and trust shape whether symptoms are voiced and believed.
- CHWs and informal providers are important first-assessment nodes in many settings.
- System design must change by region and care-flow model.

## Important examples

- England: delayed metastatic cancer diagnosis after repeated back-pain presentations across GP, ED, out-of-hours, and physiotherapy contacts.
- UK: scan interpretation and follow-up failures causing delayed cancer identification.
- India: non-allopathic provider referral and free chest-x-ray intervention reduced TB diagnostic delay.
- Tanzania: median 358-day delay from first cancer symptom to diagnosis in a cancer pathway study.
- Canada: Indigenous-specific racism and triage failure illustrated by the Brian Sinclair case.
- UK: migrant screening EHR tool identified hidden conditions and missing immunization records.
