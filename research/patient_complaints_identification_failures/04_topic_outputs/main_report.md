# Main report: Research C patient complaints and experience data

## Summary

This package captures patient-voice evidence about health-system failures and successes in identifying health issues. It focuses on complaints, reviews, advocacy stories, official complaint reports, and patient-safety cases.

## Scope

The research covered public patient reviews, complaint reports, advocacy stories, safety investigations, and selected literature. The goal was to capture varied real-world patterns, not determine prevalence.

## Method

The completed research collected and organized documented examples from public sources. During packaging, the evidence was normalized into source, quote, case, fact, metric, and implication files.

## Source overview

The archive contains 39 sources, including public reviews, ombudsman reports, HDC decisions, HSSIB/AHRQ safety material, advocacy stories, and news reports.

## Key findings


# Key findings

1. Patient complaints often reveal diagnostic workflow failures that are not visible in ordinary clinical records.
2. The most common black zones in this research were: interrupted patient history, normal-test false reassurance, abnormal results not followed up, referral delays or wrong-specialty routing, and repeated visits not connected.
3. Patient language such as “dismissed,” “not listened to,” “too young,” “normal,” and “still no idea” often maps to concrete process failures.
4. Official cases show that result follow-up requires named ownership; simply receiving, viewing, or filing a result is not enough.
5. Positive examples repeatedly include time, listening, explanation, whole-person context, and continuity.
6. Low-resource settings add additional layers: test reliability, equipment validity, affordability, and availability of specialist referral.


## Important examples

- Missed PE after clear chest X-ray and discharge.
- Positive prostate cancer histology not actioned for about six months.
- Repeated GP/out-of-hours contacts before metastatic cancer diagnosis.
- Endometriosis stories where symptoms were normalized or dismissed.
- Bowel-cancer stories where blood in stool was attributed to IBS/piles or dismissed due to age.
- Positive examples where clinicians listened, explained, examined, and followed up.

## Risks and limitations

Public patient voice is valuable for workflow signals and language, but many accounts are not independently verified. Official reports are stronger for facts and accountability but often contain fewer raw quotes.
